# Sleep and memory consolidation

A growing body of research links sleep to memory. During slow-wave sleep, the
hippocampus is thought to replay recently acquired experiences, transferring
them to the neocortex for long-term storage. This process is called memory
consolidation.

Daytime naps appear to help too. In several controlled studies, participants who
took a short nap after learning a list of items recalled more of them than
participants who stayed awake. A nap of roughly ninety minutes, long enough to
include slow-wave sleep, produced the largest gains in declarative memory.

The effect is not unlimited. Sleep deprivation the night before learning impairs
the initial encoding of new information, and no amount of later sleep fully
recovers what was never encoded well in the first place.
